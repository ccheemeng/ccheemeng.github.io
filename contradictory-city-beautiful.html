Contradictory City Beautiful

Contradictory City Beautiful is a research paper written in fulfilment of a module on the history and theory of Southeast Asian architecture, analysing the designs of the hill station of Baguio during American occupation of the Philippines in relation to the agents and structural conditions surrounding its development. Wishing to appear benevolent as they chased ambitions of their manifest destiny, American colonists straddled the paradox of having to appear simultaneously imperial and not. The paper discusses Baguio’s role in maintaining this duality and how it kept the gears of American imperialism turning to chase aspirations of empire.

Contradictory City Beautiful: Daniel H. Burnham’s 1905 Baguio Plan

Revised February 21, 2022

When I realized that the Philippines had dropped into our laps, I confess I did not know what to do with them. I sought counsel from . . . Democrats as well as Republicans . . . . I went down on my knees and prayed to Almighty God for light and guidance . . . . And one night late it came to me this way . . . [:] That we could not give them [the Philippines] back to Spain – that would be cowardly and dishonorable; That we could not turn them over to France or Germany, our commercial rivals in the Orient – that would be bad business and discreditable; That we could not leave them to themselves – they were unfit for self-government . . . [;] That there was nothing left for us to do but to take them all, and to educate the Filipinos, and uplift and Christianize them and by God’s grace do the very best we could by them, as our fellow men for whom Christ also died. And then I went to bed and went to sleep . . . and the next morning I sent for the chief engineer of the War Department . . . and I told him to put the Philippines on the map of the United States . . . and there they are and there they will stay while I am President!1

Bringing an end to over three centuries of Spanish rule in the Philippines, and threatened by competing colonial powers seizing control in the region, US President William McKinley defended US annexation of the archipelago in a 1900 interview. Driven by motivations of their manifest destiny to redeem and Christianise the (majority Catholic) Filipinos, the US was anxious to distinguish itself from its Spanish predecessors and current peers in the region as a distinctly anti-colonial power. In 1900 civil governance in the form of the Taft Commission (soon evolved into the Insular Government) was instated, replacing the military government, passing laws, and instituting a veneer of democracy. An emerging need to house the growing American population tasked with administering (in both senses of the word) governance and democracy led to the Insular Government commissioning Daniel Burnham to develop plans for Philippine capital of Manila, and the “Government reservation”2 at Baguio. This essay studies the designated “summer capital” of Baguio, and its role in maintaining the American administration in the Philippines in the early twentieth century. It argues that the Baguio drafted by Burnham served as a bastion of American culture and values, analysing it through the inherent contradictions of an empire wanting to appear benevolent. Baguio served as the factory that produced values of democracy and free market ethic, which could then be bestowed upon “little brown brothers” lacking in “Anglo-Saxon principles and skills”.3

While the US had to maintain some semblance of control in the Philippines to repel other imperial powers, it also was conscious of how its occupation appeared both to anti-imperialists in the metropole and locals in the Philippines. Supplementing the implementation of the new civil government, the US employed thousands of Americans to occupy civil servant positions in the Philippines. Yet even before the transition to civil governance, the administration found the need for a retreat from the “enervating effect” of the climate on “a Northern constitution”,4 offered by the cool mountain climate of Baguio. Equally pressing were the “dissolute, drunken, and lawless Americans . . . willing to associate with low Filipino women” whose “conduct and mode of life [were] not calculated to impress the native with the advantage of American civilization”,5 tarnishing the optics the Insular Government aimed to achieve. Baguio served as a refuge from “low” Filipino culture, allowing American values to be remade and passed on to American employees, which could then therefore be effected upon the Philippine populace. Before Burnham had been commissioned, it was already decided that Baguio would be an America away from home. Instead of sending fatigued American employees and soldiers west, they could be sent up to the mountains of Baguio to recuperate. It was from the grassy knolls of Baguio that the upper echelons of colonial administration could reside, delivering democracy to the populace below in Manila.

Monumental, Beautiful City

Burnham organised “all the more important activities, including business, municipal, and Government functions, in and around the Baguio meadow”6 highlighted in Figure 1. Soon to become Burnham Park, the esplanade became the nucleus from which American culture would radiate, into the rest of the Philippines. The business town was located on “the level floor of the meadow and the gentler slopes . . . to the northwest”, municipal buildings which “[demanded] close contact with the business quarter” were situated northwest of the meadow with the business cluster, and national buildings were situated southeast of the meadow such that “the two principal groups, National and municipal, thus face one another”. Burnham emphasised that the line drawn across the two government clusters “very nearly bisects the valley and forms the natural main axis of the town. The axis has been treated as an open esplanade with a central tapis vert or greensward.”7 The organising axis of symmetry proposed by Burnham dictated a processional sequence of views framed by landscape and monumental government buildings, and best reflects the influence of the City Beautiful movement in Burnham’s plans.

In Hines, Thomas S. “The Imperial Mall: The City Beautiful Movement and the Washington Plan of 1901-1902.” Studies in the History of Art 30 (1991): 78–99. http://www.jstor.org/stable/42620453.

Developed and largely implemented in the US in the 1890s, the City Beautiful movement was an approach to organising urban environments. Recalling historical European architectural and urban precedents, the City Beautiful movement rose in response to the rapid urban expansion caused by growing urban migration, and the resulting loss of identity, image, and coherence the grotesque suburban sprawl entailed. It gained prominence through its application and elaboration in the 1893 World’s Columbian Exposition. As director of works, Burnham ensured that architecture featured prominently in the fair. With its buildings of plaster painted white, Burnham and the other members on the board of architects curated their vision of the ideal American city for the twentieth century. A central organising axis defined by an open plaza is again apparent in the exhibition’s layout. The board drafted an axis running from west to east, fronted on the west by the Administration Building, bisecting the open plaza described by the Grand Basin, and opening up on the east to Lake Michigan. Architectural historian Thomas Hines observes that given the Beaux-Arts training of many of the architects and the entire board’s acquaintance with the axial organisation dictated by the Beaux-Arts, this motif would be referenced repeatedly on different scales in the fair, and come to “dominate most City Beautiful architecture”. He notes that “City Beautiful planning was predominantly baroque in its emphasis on processional sequences of spaces and buildings arranged as a unified group”.8 Through creating central axes of organisation, Burnham hoped to bring order to the inundating suburban sprawl. Naturally, the objects that defined these axes were also brought into focus, for they framed each space in a user’s procession through the architectural environment.

In the Exposition, the board of architects situated the Administration Building opposing the statue The Republic across the Great Basin. Ornamentation by sculptors advocating the Beaux-Arts and a uniform cornice line highlighted in Figure 3 served to frame and amplify the beauty and monumentality of this moment in a user’s procession, particularly if they passed the fair on boat through Lake Michigan. The Republic depicts a man holding a globe with an eagle perched upon it in his right hand, and a staff labelled “liberty” in his left hand. Together with the Administration Building representing the role of governance in the city, aspirations of the US seizing their manifest destiny to liberate the world were fronted in Burnham’s spatial organisation of the fair.

Taking precedence from his work in the Exposition, Burnham understood the significance of crafting such monumental views and the symbols and ideals they necessarily engendered. Of his plans for Baguio, he remarked that the “Government buildings . . . should be so located and so treated in their approaches and surroundings as to make clear their preëminence over all other buildings of the city.” In line with the Insular Government’s campaign of impressing the benefits of “modern” governance upon their colonial subjects, Burnham’s plan for Baguio communicated reverence for democracy by orienting municipal and national administrative buildings about the prime open meadow of Baguio. In addition, he levelled the topography where necessary as he organised road networks “to carry through the lines of the streets to commanding points on the hillsides and thus permit the location of monumental buildings where they command a view down neighboring streets, and count for their full value as an important element in the general effect.”9 Just as the collocation of the Administration Building and The Republic in the World’s Columbian Exposition conveyed dreams of the benevolent US leading the uncivilised Orient to salvation, Burnham’s curation of monumental views for the American or Filipino elite visitor to Baguio, à la City Beautiful, conveyed the Insular Government’s dreams of spreading the grace of democracy to their “little brown brothers”.

Despite Burnham’s intentions, the contradictions of propagating democracy through imposing Western forms plague his plans, some of which might not have been lost on Burnham himself. The hill station concept itself harkened to similar projects by their colonial counterparts, from Da Lat in French Vietnam to Shimla in British India, counterparts the Americans were eager to distinguish themselves from. Creating an exclusionary space for colonial administrators and only granting access to elite Filipinos also closely resembled the Intramuros of their Spanish predecessors. While the Americans displaced the Spanish and dismantled monuments to religion through which colonial power was administered, government buildings replaced the church, the town square replaced the poblacion,10 and these new monuments to democracy became the vehicle through which colonial power packaged in bureaucracy was administered. Peter Hall also notes militaristic influences on the City Beautiful movement: “Haussmann’s reconstruction of Paris under Napoleon III and the almost simultaneous construction of the Vienna Ringstrasse were its classic models”.11 The widening of Paris’s streets allowed Napoleon’s military to easily manoeuvre the city and hindered the construction of barricades favoured by revolutionaries and insurrectionists. Haussmann himself described his Parisian reconstruction as the “evisceration of old Paris, the district of riots, of barricades”.12 Indeed, military presence in the Philippines persisted before and long after the conception of Burnham’s plans. “The inauguration of complete civil government in the Philippines, occurring as it did simultaneously with the muster out of the twenty-four volunteer regiments of infantry . . . resulted in a great number of the personnel of such regiments being appointed to civil positions”,13 noted then Secretary of War Elihu Root in 1903. Even as military personnel were slowly phased out of civil office, military presence in the region remained long after to counter insurrectionist Philippine forces. Yet most overtly counter to claims of benevolent assimilation were the displacement of the local Baguio population in the implementation of Burnham’s plan and the cultural elision it entailed.

Culture of Division

In investigating Benguet for prospects of establishing a “recuperative station”, assigned members of the Taft Commission found Baguio: “an extensive highland region, peopled by a friendly, harmless tribe”.14 These were the Igorot people, whose nipa houses dotted the knolls of Baguio and the larger Cordillera mountain range. In Baguio, the Taft commission reported 174 locals inhabiting forty-nine houses. Susceptible to “vermin and insect life”, it was cheaper to burn and rebuild these impermanent dwellings that lasted “for perhaps five years” than repair them.15 Contrary to the division of functions integral to American culture and by extension, American administration, the spatial overlap of functions was apparent in the houses encountered by the Taft Commission, who reported of animals occupying the “meter of elevation” created by the raising of the houses above ground by stilts, and a single room which served both as a kitchen and space for sleep. In both the Kalinga and Bontok houses (vernacular forms prevalent in the Cordillera region), spaces of production like the kitchen abut, overlap, or merge with spaces of consumption, where recreation and socialisation might occur. Space for sleep is delineated, if at all, by a mat, a slight elevation in floor level, or occasionally, by a porous interior wall. Such a spatial organisation spoke to the broader organisation of Filipino culture as one that did not as distinguish clearly between production, consumption, and socialisation. Yet these nipa houses were a scar in the landscape of the City Beautiful envisioned by the Insular Government. The Taft Commission reported “the great filth that [prevailed]” and the “large number of beings that at times [inhabited]” these houses such that they were “transformed into a veritable beasts’ den”.16 Frail unsightly dwellings rising and falling throughout the landscape offended the beautiful backdrop upon which monumental concrete symbols of liberty were erected, and thus had to go.

In displacing the nipa houses and the local population, the Insular Government simultaneously did away with a culture that did not clearly delineate between spheres of public and private, domestic and labour,17 consumption and production. Eroding the “friendly, harmless” Cordilleran population from Baguio allowed Burnham to install government buildings where there were once nipa houses. More than achieving a veneer of a beautiful democracy-loving city, Burnham could organise Baguio to facilitate the division of labour, separating business from municipal governance from national governance from residence from recreation. Through this division of labour, Baguio served as the gateway from which free market ideology would permeate into the Philippines, and how the country would eventually be subsumed into the current global hegemony of capitalism.

Economic Restructuring

Under the framework provided by Burnham, the marketplace proved to be another site of contention that the Insular Government found the need to make beautiful. While the colonial administration employed inspectors to ensure hygiene standards of marketplace vendors18 to combat cholera, smallpox, and other ailments, Rebecca McKenna observes that the sanitation of the marketplace extended beyond dispelling disease to include the same spatial separation of function discussed earlier. While marketplaces were often used to care for children and house livestock in addition to selling goods, the authorities sought to “purge” the product from, and “strip” it of its history and “human and environmental relationships”. In ensuring that the marketplace was purely a site of commerce where production did not occur, the sanitation of the marketplace “diminished the likelihood” of being swindled, encouraging the Philippine populace to divorce prior notions of trade and acquisition, and vest their faith in the free market.19 The power (and danger) of the free market was such that all items that entered it were objectified, commodified, and placed on a level field of comparison via one simple notion of numerical value.

In tandem with the beautification of the marketplace and separation of production and consumption, the Insular Government sought to extend the free market ethic to their administration of the Philippines, allowing them to naturalise bureaucratic exploitation under the guise of fairness. Tasked with the “incorporation” of the Philippines “into a sphere of economic control”, the Bureau of Insular Affairs, through preferentially “weaving together distinct systems of social order and political economy”, generated a climate for the exploitation of the Filipinos. By applying the same free market ethics that Burnham’s plans embodied, they could appear to be “merely tallying accounts, recording information, compiling statistics, and writing legal briefs”,20 while effectively dispossessing locals, acquiring land and assets, and displacing Cordilleran natives from their homes. An exercise of soft power under the guise of simple law and economy once again differentiated the US from its colonial peers, promulgating the image of its genuine interest in “educating” and “uplifting” its colonial subjects. Such was the climate of the Philippines when Burnham docked in Manila for his survey of the region, and such was the climate that Burnham’s plans propagated, long after he set back for the metropole, never to set foot in the Philippines again.

While Burnham’s Baguio is apparent as a summer capital for the recuperation of colonial administrators from the miasma of the lowlands, a deeper analysis elucidates its functions in furthering US ambitions in the Philippines, and sheds light on the context fraught with contradictions that it was developed in. Baguio was a bastion of democracy, but far more than that, it served as a model for which the Philippines could be made beautiful. Given the US’s preoccupation with anti-imperialist sentiments in the metropole and rival imperial powers in the region, it always prioritised the optics of occupation. Through repeated beautification, purification, and aestheticisation, Burnham’s Baguio plan both directly helped and provided precedent for the Insular Government to straddle the curious enigma of being simultaneously imperial and not. Through the summer capital of Baguio, America was simultaneously military and civil, democratic and bureaucratic, and exploitative and just. One thing is indisputable: the Philippines was no longer ugly, but beautiful.
